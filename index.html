About This Instrument
The real thing

A gong isn't one sound. Strike a bronze disc and hundreds of modes wake up at once — inharmonic frequencies that bloom, beat against each other, and slowly fade at different rates. No two gongs are alike. Shape, thickness, and alloy determine which modes ring loudest and how long they linger.

Friction mallets & flumies

Not all gongs are struck. Flumies — rubber-tipped friction mallets — are dragged across the surface. The friction excites sustained resonance, like a bow on a string or a finger on a wine glass rim. Smaller heads sing higher modes; larger ones find the deep drones. The drag gesture in this app simulates exactly this.

What's underneath

Every sound you hear is built from scratch — no samples. 14 sine oscillators per voice, each tuned to a specific modal frequency, with independent amplitudes and decay rates. Two oscillators per mode with a slight detune create the natural beating. An FM noise layer adds the shimmer of real metal.

The five shapes aren't cosmetic. Each defines a unique set of partial ratios — the mathematical fingerprint that makes a chau sound nothing like a bell. The four alloys shift decay time, brightness, and noise character.

The sand

The Chladni visualization is computed from Bessel functions — the actual vibrational modes of a circular membrane. Each mode pushes sand away from its vibrating regions. With a single mode, you'd see clean nodal lines. But a gong excites many modes at once — their forces overlap, interfere, and flow, creating the swirling, breathing patterns you see. Real physics, just beautifully complicated.

The wave

Behind the gong surface, a 140×140 finite-difference wave simulation runs in real time — a 2D physics engine computing how energy ripples across the disc after each strike. The visual shimmer on the metal is the wave field itself.
